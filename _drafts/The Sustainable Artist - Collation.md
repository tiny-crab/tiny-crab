We’re enraptured with the myth of the tortured artist. Somebody who is willing to throw so much away in pursuit of a masterpiece. Somebody who is a ‘real’ artist only if they doggedly pursue success at any cost. It’s not limited to art - we tell stories about legendary business owners, athletes, and spiritual leaders who are deeply dedicated to their purpose. They sacrifice in order to succeed.

Less popular is the concept of the sustainable artist’s practice. Somebody who makes art in their spare time. It’s not the primary way they make an income, if it makes an income at all. Somebody who doesn’t describe themselves as an artist when others ask what they do. They take breaks from their practice when they need it.

Can the sustainable artist succeed? Can the sustainable artist make great art?

What lies on the path from creative acts, to art, to _great_ art?

### What does the sustainable artist’s practice look like?

**It looks like Work**

The economy of today dictates a level of divergent extremism in people wishing to create art. To pay bills, artists veer into one of two lanes: make money with your art, or don’t. Once an artist has committed themselves to making money with their art, they’re locked into a knock-down drag-out fight to find a way to survive. It’s no wonder that the myth of the tortured artist enraptures us. It’s the same Protestant work ethic that drags the bloated corpse of the janitor-turned-CEO-story through every generation. This the stagnant and putrid lifeblood of American values.

On the contrary, if an artist finds themselves not making any money with their art, they turn to another source of income in order to live. This alternative source of income usually falls prey to the same pressures as if art was the primary income. It becomes one of their highest priorities (and may well need to be) in order for them to stay employed and pay their bills. Suddenly, that artist who decided to take an alternative income isn’t practicing their art anymore.

Pressfield reinforces this contrast between the amateur and the professional against each other:

> The amateur plays for fun. The professional plays for keeps. The amateur plays part-time, the professional full-time. The amateur is a weekend warrior. The professional is there seven days a week.

The binary that’s rendered from the economic context is there, plain as day. Pressfield reveres the professional who can dedicate so much to their craft. It might appear that he buys into this worldview of just two modes: your art practice is your highest priority, or it gets buried in your backyard next to your pet hamster.

But Pressfield doesn’t conflate the professional with the tortured artist. He actually encourages artists to practice like they do with their day jobs, implicating that the two can coexist, and proposes these tenets for the professional artist:

> 1. We show up every day. We might do it only because we have to, to keep from getting fired.

1. We show up no matter what. We might do it only so as not to let down our co-workers, or for other, less noble reasons.
    
3. We stay on the job all day. Our minds may wander, but our bodies remain at the wheel.
    
4. We are committed over the long haul. Next year we may go to another job, another company […] Until we hit the lottery, we are part of the labor force.
    
5. The stakes for us are high and real. This is about survival, feeding our families, educating our children.
    
6. We accept remuneration for our labor. We’re not here for fun. We work for money.
    
7. We do not over-identify with our job. We may take pride in our work […] but we recognize that we are not our job descriptions.
    
8. We master the technique of our job.
    
9. We have a sense of humor about our job.
    
10. We receive praise or blame in the real world.
    

Pressfield admires the detachment that artists have with their day job, and takes note of the ability with which people can perform them. Pressfield believes that art is best practiced as **Work**.

The tortured artist is not the professional that he describes. The tortured artist is desperately committed to their one opportunity, their one project that defines them. The tortured artist is over-identified with their work. If it fails, they fail. If their art dies, so do they. The tortured artist is an amateur. This isn’t a good model for anybody’s mental health or the quality of their art.

Their practice and output will inevitably fail while attached to the end result. Pressfield advocates for attachment to the process, not the result. He says “the amateur composer will never write [their] symphony because [they are] overly invested in its success and over-terrified of its failure. The amateur takes it so seriously it paralyzes [them].”

The professional has a level of seriousness seen in their dedication to the craft. And yet, Pressfield calls out explicitly that artists also should “have a sense of humor about our job”. This seems contradictory at first glance. As Pressfield puts it bluntly:

> You don’t hear [the amateur] bitching, “This fucking trilogy is killing me!” Instead, [they don’t] write [their] trilogy at all.

The amateur sees the failures of their projects as reflections of their own capability and worth. They can’t have a good humor about the failures of their project because of their attachment to it. And yet that same person could shatter and spill a bottle of olive oil while working as a grocery clerk, and describe it as a funny story to their partner after they’ve clocked out. Artists must vie for that level of detachment with their art, knowing that great work is not guaranteed even for artists at the top of their game. Artists must let themselves make mediocre art, and release it all the same. Artists must let themselves fuck up, and laugh about it later.

**It looks like Play**

The desire to engage with new artistic mediums wanes as creatives reach adulthood. It can be difficult to start over with a new hobby and a new set of skills. Adults have a wealth of experience to draw upon. Their internal world is much more developed and as a result, more constrained. It doesn’t feel good to struggle to draw a portrait when one knows what it’s “supposed” to look like.

The easiest time to start learning an art form is in childhood. Children don’t always have a notion of what things are “supposed” to be like. Their internal world is more loose and this results in a freer expression of it. Their skills and perception grow in tandem. As creative adults, it can be difficult to reconcile the large gap between taste and technique. The lack of technique can be disappointing and frustrating to creative adults, because their taste and perception tells them that their work isn’t “supposed” to look unrefined and unskilled.

This is why Rubin asks artists to approach their art as **Play.** He believes that it’s better to approach it with the openness of a child, and a willingness to explore. What does it look like when artists treat art with a playful mindset?

> In play, there are no stakes. No boundaries. No right or wrong. No quotas for productivity. It’s an uninhibited state where your spirit can run free.

Play invites a posture toward art that is fun, and entirely personal. It is up to the artist to find the things that are fun to them, just as a child explores all their toys to find the one that piques their interest in the current moment. A child doesn’t play for somebody else’s pleasure or what somebody else perceives as correct. A child doesn’t play if it’s not fun anymore - they move on to something else.

Adults aren’t as free as children in their non-creative lives. There are bills to pay, mouths to feed, and others to connect with. There are more responsibilities. This is why adults need to protect and safeguard their art. Their artistic practice may be the only space where they get to truly do what they want*.* Rubin calls this out as a danger:

> Seriousness saddles the work with a burden. […] Putting importance on the work too soon stirs up instincts of caution.

He believes that the only way to capture those sparks of inspiration are through this open, playful mindset without a plan. Walking blindly through the artistic process, and listening to the inner world’s response to it.

Pressfield imbues his book with an urgency, an imperative to get to Work: your art. It’s a shout of Labeoufian ecstasy to **just do it.** To Pressfield, art is do or die. It’s a stark contrast to Rubin’s caution to artists, that “…it’s a privilege … We’re not being ordered to do this. If we’d rather not do it, let’s not do it.”

Rubin accepts that art is a vulnerable and challenging endeavor for many artists.

> The sensitivity that allows [artists] to make the art is the same vulnerability that makes them more tender to being judged. […] If a creator is so afraid of judgment that they’re unable to move forward, it might be that the desire to share the work isn’t as strong as the desire to protect themselves.

**Is sustainable art mediocre?**

- **How should art exist for the mediocre?**
    - Rubin talks about complexity / simplicity and how complexity might be lauded as amateurs attempt to make great works. He suggests that three simple chords from an amateur might be considered “greater” than something complex written by a virtuoso. But that something might always resonate with somebody. We’re still defining greatness by HOW MANY people are interested in a thing, which reduces down to popularity as a measure of greatness.
    - Pressfield suggests that amateurs are not passionate enough: “if [they] did, [they] would not pursue it as a sideline, distinct from [their] ‘real’ vocation”.
    - Perhaps in a time long past, when the only music or poetry or theater performance was live and from a traveling caravan, mediocre artists had a place. These days, when anybody can consume the content from every talented and popular artist of our time, what’s the point of making mediocre art? Is stagnation acceptable?
    - Rubin: “Ultimately, the act of self-expression isn’t really about you. Most who choose the artist’s path don’t have a choice. We feel compelled to engage, as if by some primal instinct, the same force that calls turtles toward the sea after hatching in the sand.”
- Does every artist want to be great? Are all the mediocre ones content, or are they also striving? Why haven’t they become great yet?
