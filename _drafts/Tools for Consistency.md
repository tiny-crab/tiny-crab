I’ve written before about how ADHD manifests in me as inconsistency. Some days I have unlimited energy to solve every problem, answer every question. Other days I feel tethered to a drip feed of dopamine, unable to focus on anything around me.

I discovered a handful of tools this year that has helped smooth out these peaks and valleys. This isn’t to say that these are the best tools in their domain. But these are the ones that I’ve latched onto and helped me continue to do what I want to do - even if I’m inconsistent at pursuing it.

## Lunatask: Blending Work and Life

I’ve spent the least time with [Lunatask](https://lunatask.app/), but it’s changed the game for me the most.

In the past, I split up my days in two different spaces: work and life. Work tasks were completed between the hours of 9a and 5p. Life tasks were completed outside of that window. I tried to avoid overlap - not distracting myself from work during work hours, and not working early or late to take away from my “free” time.

It was difficult to always maintain this strict boundary. I found myself overwhelmed with logistical needs at the end of each day, and hated the work → household tasks → personal tasks → hobbies → fun things hierarchy. I don’t always have the most energy at the end of a day of work.

Not only this, but it wasn’t like I was always being productive during those 8 work hours. I take breaks pretty frequently throughout the day, but I would only utilize them as “rest” from work. A lot of free time snuck in the cracks there, but because it was so broken up it didn’t ever really feel well-utilized. In order to fit more into a day, I started to blend my work and life time together.

I’ve been utilizing my breaks at work as a way to get personal or household tasks done, or even spend time doing fun, intentional breaks instead of just zoning out on the internet. Lunatask has helped this by both:

- centralizing my tasks and blending work, household, and personal into a single list
- using the timeblocking feature to make time for everything.

**Centralizing tasks**

I’m lucky enough to work from home and be able to spend time doing chores, completing personal or household errands and tasks, and not be supervised or scrutinized as to how I spend my time. Because of this, I’m able to do tasks in order of _my_ overall priority, instead of just my work’s priority.

While at work, it seems obvious to prioritize your work tasks in order of how important they are at work. But as I started to challenge my conception of boundaries between work tasks and personal tasks, I found that there were some things that were _more_ important to me in the immediate day-to-day to complete over my work. And this never really came with the sacrifice of _not_ meeting things at work. In fact, it meant that I could double-down on my working time, and leave more time in the evenings to rest and refresh for the next day. If anything, I’ve found that doing this has helped me be more consistent at completing daily tasks at work.

For me, the dopamine hit I get from completing a tiny personal task (say, sending a tax document to an accountant) helps to keep my momentum, instead of “costing” me anything. I get a little rush when I realize that I saved myself some extra free time at the end of the day, now that I don’t have to complete that task anymore.

**Timeblocking**

Lunatask also has this really neat feature where you can take all your tasks for the day and slot them into specific times on your calendar. I had started to do this off and on in Google Calendar for my really busy days, but doing this every day has helped me continue to fight back against mental [time distortions](https://arc.net/l/quote/bizhhahe). You can do this manually without Lunatask, but making it a part of the default workflow when tracking work to do has made it much more sticky for me.

**Other Good Features**

- Lunatask has a default time estimate field on every todo (at 5 minute intervals, which fit perfectly into [the practice I had started earlier in the year](https://arc.net/l/quote/qpdklzwk)
- It also includes a habit tracker, which I haven’t made a _ton_ of use of yet, but I use it to track my daily journaling and Japanese studying.
- Separating task lists into “Areas of Life” and “Goals” per area has helped categorize todos but still feed them all into a single place.

## Sweepy: Habits as a Reminder

I’ve been using Sweepy for several months now, and I’ll admit - I’ve fallen in and out of habit with it over those few months. But I think that’s a lesson I’ve been learning more recently: the most important part of a habit isn’t when you’re _doing_ it. It’s when you return to a habit after failing.

- reframing habits
    - habits aren’t a dictator
    - because perfection isn’t the goal - the habit tracker is the reminder to pick back up. Not to destroy your self esteem
- tolerant streaks (wild cards, one task per day counts toward streak)
- breaking down into tiny tasks
- cleaning as an antidote to screen time
- picked up and dropped a couple of different times, currently in an upswing
- capacity per day enables no chores on weekends, a blessing that comes from being able to work from home and interleaving chores through my day
