## Sam

- his desire to never be seen as somebody who needs help come from sadie’s “betrayal” of community service and being seen as a charity case

## Sadie

- her desire for recognition and being seen coming from being ignored and neglected while her sister was in the hospital
- both sides as an analogy of the switch that’s flipped between their perspectives and what they perceive of one another - at the end when they both finally confess their insecurities, and it’s so nonchalant and anti-climactic. They both realize they’ve been holding onto these grudges or fears for so long, and now finally get to relax the grip on them because they have a life outside of that deep desire to make great things. That has passed

## Marx

- memorialization of him in games, when he didn’t get to be ghost in the chair during Hamlet as Banquo
- NPC is such an interesting concept in this book - he’s the one character I was so angry at when he wouldn’t communicate to Sam and pressure him. He didn’t act when he knew that Sam was struggling! We didn’t know his inner monologue. But Sadie and Sam are the ones that act and react to each other, while Marx just exists as a support to them. It’s so odd.
