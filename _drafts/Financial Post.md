Emotional

- financial literacy is given from our families. How we talk and think about money is dictated by our parents, and our parents parents (or other significant guardians / mentors)
    - That said, we pick up on things in childhood and twist or perceive them as children, where our parents or guardians may actually think in different terms if these are concepts they learned as adults.
    - Mine: spending on little things and trinkets is useless. Saving is important. If you don’t have enough money for retirement when you’re 55 you’ll instantly die. Stability stability stability.
    - Courtney’s: feast or famine, whiplash in spending / scrimping, beans and toast everyday forever. Only one thing can be saved for or spent on at a time.
    - Both of us: money is scary
- Important convos we’ve had
    - How do we know how much we can spend on ourselves? Do we need to talk to each other for every little thing?
    - Collaboration on spending tracking and visibility, I didn’t want to be the only one who knew how much money was in the bank. I wanted Courtney to be able to make decisions independently, while paradoxically wanting her to always make the same decisions that I would (impossible)
    - Guilt around shared income and spending
    - Difficulty planning ahead for long term goals (house, children, business)
    - Difficult to have conversations at all about finances without unearthing fear and lots of emotions around guilt / shame, which made those conversations all the more difficult
    - What happens if we run over our budget?
    - Cash envelope strategy never felt like it worked for basics, because what happens if we ran out?
    - Over-focus on categorization and details, with little understanding of high level breakdown

- Financial planning for a dose of reality without invoking shame - just do what you can with what you have. Everybody is in a different position.
- Transaction categorizing with RightCapital in the past and now Copilot

Conceptual:

- “base” expenses - these are big, static charges that could instantly change overnight if you move, buy or sell a car, take out a loan, etc.
- Lifestyle expenses - these are monthly, highly fluid budgets that are unlikely to change unless you make drastic changes to your lifestyle (again, unlikely)
- Values-based spending - these are pools of money (not a budget) that you draw from to pay for “extras”. What is “extra” is totally up to you! It helps to think about things you might be doing every month vs what might come up only as needed or only once every few months
    - That said, these should be able to be quickly reallocated depending on how your priorities change, like if you want to save up for a special vacation, you can just move $$s around!

How does money flow from your income and into each of these concepts?

Add together base and lifestyle to get your total monthly expenses. In a pinch, this is how money you’d burn each month while you have a loss of income. All values-based spending should be able to be paused if such a thing happened.

From your direct deposit, set aside this much from your monthly pay. Then you can split the remainder among your values-based spending. The remainder after that is what you can use to save, invest, etc.

Mechanical

Credit card for all expenses

Checking account to pay off credit card in full at end of month

Savings accounts for emergency, anomalous, and all values based buckets (allowances, travel, gifts, etc)

Direct deposit is split up into:

- Checking account to pay for all base + lifestyle expenses (rather, pay off the credit card that accumulates almost all of it)
- Push some money into emergency funds and anomalous spending
    - emergency being for worst case scenario, anomalous spending being mechanics, vets, ER, house work, etc.
- Push money into investment accounts
- Push rest of money into values-based spending buckets
- Push any leftovers into Unallocated to show how much you have “left over” if you spend according to your budget. This can hypothetically go anywhere, but probably best to invest or put into emergency and anomalous.
