## What are some moments I want to remember from 2024?

This year was probably one of the most difficult years I can remember. Courtney and I experienced a lot of upheaval, both on paper and under the surface.

In January, we decided to move down to San Diego in March. That was probably much too quick, but we thought if we were to wait for 2025, we might have never moved. This caused a fair amount of financial and relational stress between Courtney and I.

We were unpacking our giant shipping crate in San Diego when I got a call from my mom that my Nana’s health was deteriorating rapidly and she would probably be gone soon. Courtney and I flew back up to Seattle to see her after just two weeks in our new place. She passed the second week of April.

In July, Courtney’s stepdad suddenly began having multiple psychogenic seizures daily. He spent a couple weeks in the hospital, then underwent physical therapy in a convalescent home, and has since needed 24 hour monitoring. Courtney’s been helping take care of him nearly 40 hours a week.

At the beginning of this year, my anxiety transformed from something largely contained within my mind to deep-rooted bodily panic. I went to the ER a couple of times due to my panic attacks, and have had a handful of weeks throughout the year where I barely ate. I used to always know what I was anxious about, but these days it can be really hard to pin down.

Okay and the election result was pretty fucked, too.

It’s obvious that there’s a lot of things I wish I could forget from this year. I feel like I’ve aged a lot, and I already felt old for my age to begin with.

Looking back from today, I read over this and sigh. It could have been worse, sure, but it was a pretty shit year. And yet the thing that makes me feel most adult about all of it is that I feel a little proud of how Courtney and I handled all of this. And I think about the things that have supported me throughout this year. There’s a lot to be grateful for.

As a child, especially growing up in a Protestant household, I felt pressure to be grateful. “Things could always be worse”! I was frustrated by this for a long long time. I was grateful, for a lot of things, but it didn’t ever seem like it was _enough_ to balance out the shit parts of my life. I even wrote about this two years ago in [another blog post](https://zachcrabtree.com/2023/12/11/simplicity.html):

> Gratitude has been enshrined into modern society’s ethos over the last decade. I’m resistant to gratitude as a self-practice. Gratitude can be used by oneself to dismiss their own negative feelings. It can be weaponized by people who are rankled by others’ negative emotions. If mindfulness asks you to “think about the things you’re thinking about”, gratitude asks you to “think harder about things you don’t like”. American Gratitude asks us to think positive. A busy street suffocated with noise and exhaust might have a silver lining if you position yourself juuuuuust right.

I don’t necessarily rely on gratitude to counteract grief these days. But while reflecting on all the things that happened this year, I do feel a sense of “it could have been worse”, and it makes me grateful that it wasn’t. When I was a kid, “it could have been worse” probably just meant “I’m sure glad a meteor didn’t fall out of the sky and explode my house”. This isn’t really a consolation as much as it is a dismissal.

These days, “it could have been worse” means “I’m sure glad that Courtney and I were able to support each other throughout all these rough patches” and “I’m glad that my friends are around to listen to me vent about all this BS” and “I’m glad that I have this little moment, eating a California burrito, to help me for a single moment not feel crushed by the weight of what comes next and what’s happening just out of view”.

And even then, there’s _so many_ things in 2024 that I’m extremely, _extremely_ proud of, excited for, and overjoyed about. There’s a lot of things that just… happened, but may be a start of something greater in 2025.

So much has happened. Some good, some bad, some terrible. Few things were “resolved” or wrapped up. There’s still a lot of questions around what’s next for me, for us, for our family. We’re still floating in the inexorable river of time but I’m starting to trust that I know how to swim - or at least, tread water.

## What are some things I’m proud of doing in 2024?

### Productivity

**Efficiency**

This year has been more slow and steady progress toward more efficient productivity. I’ve experimented with some more tools and processes to improve how much I can get done at work and at home.

I’ve been getting a lot better at circumventing the main challenge that comes with my ADHD: the gut reaction that a task is going to take _forever_ when it’s actually just going to take a couple minutes. I wrote about this some more in my [ADHD tips post](https://zachcrabtree.com/2024/11/06/adhd.html), but I’ll sum up here what’s really helped.

The realization that _so many things_ can be done in less than 10 minutes has changed my life. You can clean a toilet in less than 10 minutes. In fact, if you’re well prepared, you can clean an entire bathroom in less than 10 minutes. You can vacuum a room in less than 10 minutes. You can write an email in less than 10 minutes. You get the point.

I realized that the many small tasks that piled up for me in the past (since I tend to procrastinate) can be checked off so quickly. I can do 6 or 8 of these tasks in just one hour! This might be… ridiculously obvious for anybody reading this who isn’t neurodivergent, but it’s been a godsend to me.

I’ve been able to work in small tasks like cleaning, cooking, organizational tasks at home throughout my days at work. And I’ve been able to crush lots of random things at work that I might have shrugged off in the past as too much work.

**Job**

I also attempted to rebalance my time at work and tried to stop caring as much about what I “should” do and more about what I **personally** thought was most important. Counterintuitively, this switch contributed directly to my promotion this summer.

I’ve only ever gotten title bumps from changing companies before, and never been promoted internally. I’m really proud of this. I went from nearly getting fired as a junior engineer at Amazon a few years ago and thinking I wasn’t cut out for the industry, to hitting my stride as a senior engineer and having an influence on the organization around me today. It’s kind of mindblowing and I wish I could go back to my younger self to pat him on the back and tell him that he’s in the right profession.

### Community

Before getting promoted, I was doing some soul-searching about what I wanted out of my job. I was able to discuss these feelings with people at my company and people that I previously worked with. It is so lovely to have a group of people that I can seek advice from and learn from. I know so many people who model the skills I want to learn, and it’s reassuring to even just vent to them.

This year, outside of work, it was such a pleasure to stay in close contact with so many friends from Seattle despite the distance. I was worried that I would feel isolated in San Diego, but I have been able to have a long, intimate chat with somebody on the phone every two weeks or so since moving. This has been fuel for me to keep pushing and trying new things.

I knew that when I moved, I knew I wouldn’t be completely alone - Courtney and I have had friends down here for years now. But I knew it’d be a little different, because they’re “Courtney’s” friends, not mine. I wanted to seek out other connections, but working in a remote environment for a company not based in San Diego doesn’t make it very easy to meet other people.

I joined a couple of different groups down here to connect with like-minded people: the International Game Developer’s Association, a mixed-media artist cohort called The Table, and attended a birdwatching group walk. It was tough going into these spaces and purposefully seeking out friendships instead of activity time. But I walked away with a several acquaintances and a couple friends that I’m hoping will continue to develop over the next year.

### Fear

**Driving**

I never really engaged in these sorts of communities on my own in Seattle because it was tough to get around without a drivers license.

This year I’m really proud to finally have my license for the first time! And I’ll say that I’m a pretty good driver too, despite the little experience I have!

I was afraid of getting my license, and procrastinated on it for a long time because of that fear. When I was just learning how to drive when I was 15, I was with a friend (who was driving) and we hit a kid who jumped off the sidewalk on his scooter, right in front of the car. I swore off driving then, but have been in a couple of other car accidents and seen more than one pedestrian get hit by a car in front of my eyes. I was worried that I would make a mistake while driving and hit somebody and hurt them. I worry even now that I’ll make a mistake while driving and injure the passengers in my car.

In the end, another fear won out over the fear of driving - fear that I couldn’t take Courtney to the hospital if something happened to her. After I needed to go to the ER in January because I thought I was having a heart attack, I realized that if Courtney was injured I wouldn’t be able to take care of her. After that, I focused on _just_ passing the written test first, and not thinking too hard about driving or getting my license. Once I had my permit, it was easier to try driving for the first time. And once I drove around with my permit enough, it was easier to think about the driving test to get my license. Not thinking too far ahead really helped me focus on the next step and not let all the fear wash over me.
