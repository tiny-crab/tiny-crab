For as long as I can remember, I’ve struggled with finishing projects.

Countless ideas left half-baked, forgotten about, or dropped because I grew tired of working on them.

In starting a Substack, I’ve gotten afraid of publishing posts (especially like weekly updates) because I’m already starting to ask myself: “When am I going to stop?” in other words: when am I going to fail?

### The Creative Act

- Three phases: Experimentation, Craft, and Completion
    - [In Depth: FFSTrainGame](https://www.notion.so/In-Depth-FFSTrainGame-8dee54e731f14243aaa5bdd70d8e58bf?pvs=21) - a goal that I went in thinking I would “finish” and had a subconscious idea of what that is. I didn’t meet that idea, and thus it was easy for me to feel like a failure.

I am afraid of starting things because I’ve started a bunch of projects that don’t meet my learned standards of what a “finished” project is.

- In the communities online that I have lurked on for years, prolificness (prolifery?) is the number 1 goal. Do a bunch of projects to learn from them. _Just finish things._ but these people never talk about what that finish part means. AND - what’s baked into that statement is an assumption that your intent is **to be good**. Or to be skilled, or recognized, or to become successful in some way.
    
- Tommy challenged me by asking: is finishing a project creatively satisfying to me? What’s the thing that’s creatively satisfying to me when participating in a project?
    
    - It was relatively easy for me to answer:
        - For game dev, the most satisfying thing is getting to design and solve a small problem within the game I’m working on. Getting a new feature into my game within a short amount of time (ideally, one evening) is immensely satisfying. I love recording a demo gif of what I completed, no matter how small.
        - For music, the most satisfying thing is when I can engage with music in this hypnotic, trance-inducing improvisation. I love when I can spend even 15 minutes completely engrossed at whatever improvisation, whether based off another song or created from scratch in that moment.
    - What was difficult for me to answer:
        - Is finishing a project satisfying to me?
            - Is publishing a game satisfying?
            - Is publishing my music satisfying?
        - It seems like my idea of what “finishing” a thing was to publish it to the public, no strings attached. No “this is a first draft” or “ this is just a prototype” or “this is a sketch I put together in 15 minutes”. I had to be ready to face the audience and their opinions about what I’m putting together.

What’s the alternative?

Reflection. Turning the question from “is this good enough?” to “how do I feel about this?” or “what do I want to do next with this thing?”

Rick Rubin talks about the river flowing; if you’re dedicated to a project and feel like you need to make it perfect and you refuse to move on before it is perfect, you’re robbing yourself of the days that you could be spending working on something new. A project is only a snapshot of where you were at any given time. If you spend more time on it, it will be different. Not better, not worse. Only different - just as you are different.

I feel like with even writing here, I’ve been stuck on this project idea for a couple of weeks. I say:

- “It’s not worth publishing, because it’s not long enough”
- “It’s not worth publishing, because it’s just my opinion. What’s the point?”
- “It’s not worth publishing, I could do more research and pull from other sources to assert my point.”

Better to unblock the river and move forward. No project is a magnum opus, or the pinnacle of your life’s work. It’s just a snapshot of where you are in time. A memorial that you can look back to in the future.
